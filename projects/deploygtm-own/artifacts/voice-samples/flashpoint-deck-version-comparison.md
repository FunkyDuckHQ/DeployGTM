## Version 1 — Early Read / Schoolfield

The oldest version is more conversational and exploratory. It opens as "thinking out loud" and spends more time on why Flashpoint is a real bet: product differentiation, founder/background credibility, existing pull, and agency wedge logic.

Useful traits:
- More explicit reasoning chain.
- More willingness to pressure-test assumptions.
- Strong operator instinct around market-size skepticism and ICP discipline.
- The recurring revenue idea is already present: accumulated research memory is the durable value.

What later versions improve:
- Less background exposition.
- Cleaner slide thesis lines.
- More compact revenue math.
- Sharper first-30-days execution sequence.

## Version 2 — Some Thoughts / Stegenga (3)

This version is much closer to the final style. It has stronger section architecture, clearer page titles, and more polished language around the core thesis.

Reusable moves:
- "The current revenue is a map."
- Agency first, while enterprise work continues in parallel.
- Revenue math framed as project-only, agency-only, or hybrid.
- Recurring revenue separated into repeat services, SaaS today, and the durable platform bet.
- Execution framed as selling while tightening the system.

What still gets tightened later:
- Some lines are still more explanatory than necessary.
- The final deck becomes a little less branded and a little more personal/direct.
- The close becomes more learning-oriented: where to double down and where the deck was wrong.

## Final Version — Some Thoughts / Stegenga

The final version is the cleanest expression of the voice:
- Humble but decisive.
- Commercially direct.
- Less explanatory than the oldest version.
- Still grounded in concrete operating questions.
- Focused on what repeats, what becomes recurring, and where to start selling.

## What This Teaches The System

- Drafts should start wide enough to preserve reasoning, then compress toward the real decision.
- Do not overfit to final phrasing only; the older versions reveal how Matthew thinks.
- The strongest final artifact keeps the useful caveat but cuts the extra proof once the recommendation is obvious.
- Matthew's best deck voice combines three things: honest uncertainty, simple business math, and a first operating sequence.
- The system should retain earlier drafts when available because manual edits and version evolution are high-value voice evidence.
