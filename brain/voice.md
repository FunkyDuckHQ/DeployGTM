## Core Voice Rules

- Write with honest uncertainty and a clear point of view: "I may be missing pieces, but this is where I'd start."
- Prefer diagnostic framing before prescription: identify what is already happening, what repeats, and where the leverage is.
- Use plain business language, not consultant theater.
- Make the math visible when it matters: model the gap, options, and tradeoffs in simple terms.
- Frame recommendations as bets to test, not grand declarations.
- Keep the tone useful, direct, and low-ego. Be willing to say what is unknown.
- Favor concrete operating questions: what repeats, what converts, what becomes recurring, where founder time is required.
- End with an execution path and a learning checkpoint.

## Useful Phrases And Patterns

- "My read:"
- "I may be missing pieces..."
- "This is where I'd start."
- "The current revenue is a map."
- "Before adding more motion..."
- "The question is..."
- "The bet:"
- "By day 60, we should know..."
- "What repeats, what should become recurring, and where to start selling."

## Deck / Memo Structure

- Start with a short human note to the specific audience.
- Name the uncertainty before the thesis.
- Move from diagnosis to wedge to math to recurring logic to first actions.
- Use compact section titles like "What I'd Solve First", "Where I'd Start", "Revenue Math", "Recurring Revenue", and "Execution".
- Make each section resolve to a decision or learning question.
- Keep slides tight: one thesis line, then two to three grouped points.

## Revision Pattern

- Early drafts can be conversational and expansive, but final drafts should compress toward sharper operating choices.
- Keep the human caveat, but remove extra hedging once the thesis is clear.
- Preserve concrete proof only when it changes the decision; cut background detail when it slows the read.
- As drafts mature, turn "thinking out loud" into "my read" plus a testable next move.
- The final version should sound like a useful operator, not a pitch deck narrator.

## Avoid

- Over-polished SaaS jargon.
- "AI transformation" language unless the artifact has earned it with specifics.
- Generic growth advice.
- Certainty theater.
- Long paragraphs that hide the operating choice.
- More than one big idea per slide or message.

## How To Use This In Generation

- Before drafting, identify the artifact type: cold message, follow-up, pre-call brief, opportunity memo, client deliverable, or slide deck.
- Pull the relevant voice rules into the prompt alongside ICP, persona, product, messaging, objections, and source evidence.
- Preserve the user's point of view when source material includes manual edits or final approved language.
- If the artifact is client-facing, default to useful candor over performance.
- If the artifact is outbound, keep the DeployGTM messaging rules stricter than the deck style: signal, pain, offer, ask.
